How to read the four rebound columns (both tables) — each answers "what happens after buying on a dip?", measured over every day in the stock's history it closed ≥20% below its own high — any depth (a day it's down 50% counts too; no upper cap), whether it's still falling, at the bottom, or climbing back. That's many entry days per stock (e.g. NVDA ≈ 1,770), so nothing is cherry-picked. Worked example — AVGO:

  • Dip→1y — the typical (median) one-year return across all those days, if you'd bought and held exactly 12 months. AVGO +73%.
  • Win% (first number in Win% · worst-10%) — how often that one-year return was positive. AVGO 100% — every dip-buy was up a year later.
  • worst-10% (second number) — the bad case: the return in the unluckiest 1-in-10 entries. AVGO still +15%; contrast AMD −43%, PLTR −64%.
  • vs SPY — the same median minus the S&P over identical windows (strips the bull-market tailwind — the fairest cross-name number). AVGO +55% better than the index.
  • Recov'd — of its ≥20% crashes, how many climbed back to the prior high. AVGO 13 / 14.

02Why the fair metric matters

The earlier "trough → peak" numbers were unfair two ways — they cherry-picked the best subsequent moment, and "trough → now" just reflected where each name sits in its own cycle today. The fair metric fixes both: the median 12-month return from every day the stock was ≥20% off its high — same trigger, same horizon, all instances. What it shows:

  • Pays most and reliably: AVGO (+73%, 100% hit, +15% worst-case), META (+85%, +58% vs market), CEG, CDNS, and — notably — TSM (+54%, +30% vs market, up 87% of the time). "Cheap won't bounce" was wrong.
  • Explosive but brutal-tailed: PLTR (+81% median but −64% worst-decile), AMD (−43% worst-decile), VRT (+186% but concentrated in the 2023–24 cooling boom — a one-cycle outlier, not a base rate).
  • Doesn't pay: SMCI (+14%, the only name that underperforms cash from a dip) — the old "34× rebound" was an illusion of peak-picking.
  • SK Hynix beats Micron on the same test (+51% vs +42%, 91% vs 77% hit, +2% vs −22% worst-case) — the HBM leader is the higher-quality memory play, though both ride the same cycle.

The fairest single number is "vs SPY" — it strips out the bull-market tailwind that flatters everyone. By it, the dependable market-beating rebounders are AVGO +55, META +58, CEG +41, CDNS +36, TSM +30, SKHY +25, AMZN +23; SMCI (−2), MRVL (+0), VST (+7), AMD (+9) barely beat or lag. That pushes the list toward AVGO / META / TSM and away from the froth.

Peer-reviewed backing (Consensus): large firms that drop >20% in a month earn ~+12% excess return over the next 12 months — strongest in technology — while small / service-tier names drift lower instead (Benou & Madura, 2003). That is exactly the split this table finds: deep dips in large, moated tech mean-revert; the sub-scale AVOIDs (SMCI, SentinelOne, Okta) don't. The literature's one caveat is horizon — one-day / one-week drops can keep falling (Cox & Peterson, 1994) — which is why the trigger here is a ≥20% level and the horizon a full year.

The signal is quality-gated: only PRIME / CONDITIONAL names earn a DEPLOY — FRAGILE / AVOID dips don't reliably recover (the anti-Cisco rule), so they're SKIP even when deeply down. 04Cybersecurity — AI's next demand leg

You're right that an AI world needs more security, and the peer-reviewed literature backs it: AI expands the attack surface — adversarial ML, data poisoning, prompt injection, agentic-AI risk, and a machine-identity explosion (~109 machine identities per human). Gartner sees information-security spend hitting $244B in 2026 (+13%) → $322B by 2029, explicitly driven by AI on both offense and defense.

Caveat — this sector is not dipping right now. CrowdStrike and Palo Alto rose ~95% and ~113% in Q2 2026 (their best quarter ever) on the AI-security bid, so treat this as a watchlist for when a crash comes, not a buy-today list. And the pure-plays got absorbed: Palo Alto closed its $25B CyberArk acquisition (Feb 2026), bought Chronosphere (observability) and Protect AI, so identity + observability + AI-model security now express through PANW — there's no clean public "AI-security pure-play" left.

06The two Elon names, reworked

Tesla — upgraded, with a catch that matters

The non-car story crossed from vaporware to real-but-unproven: robotaxis are genuinely driverless and paid (monitor-free since Jan 2026, multi-city), and Q2-2026 was a record 480k deliveries (+25%). The earlier take was too dismissive — credit due.

But ~90% of the $1.5T cap is optionality (the car business justifies ~$150–350B). Robotaxi is still a pilot (material revenue ~2027+), Optimus unproven, FSD far from unsupervised. The catch for a dip-buyer: unlike the 2022 −74% (a growing carmaker that recovered), so little is car earnings today that if the bets disappoint, the downside is a permanent de-rate toward the car floor (−60–80%), not a bounce.

Stance: dip-buyable as a sized position for the Elon optionality — add aggressively only on evidence (2027 robotaxi revenue in guidance, verified Optimus work), not narrative.

Now includes xAI (Grok) plus — via a $60B all-stock deal (Jun 2026, pending Q3 close) — Cursor, the ~$4B-ARR AI-coding leader. Moat is real — 34× ULA's launch mass, 10.3M Starlink subs vs Kuiper's ~380 satellites. But at ~$145 the price carries a large narrative premium: ~56% of the cap sits above fundamental value (the quantified "Elon effect"). Cursor makes the AI leg real, but at ~3% of the cap (all-stock, ~15× sales) it strengthens the story more than the price.

07The IPO calendar is a catalyst, not just a threat

What it means: expect episodic, headline-driven dips around each pricing and lockup — those are potential entry windows — rather than a sustained drain. SpaceX's Aug–Dec lockup and any OpenAI/Anthropic pricing wobble are more likely to create the dip than to threaten the durable names.
